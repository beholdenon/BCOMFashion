The Tools:

What makes a good food great? The proper instruments.
Brush up on the pot and pan particulars and get cooking.

Dutch Oven

A cylindrical heavy gauge metal or ceramic pot with a tight-fitting lid

The Details

The Dutch oven is truly an all-purpose piece of cookware, but is best known for its long, slow cooking capabilities.

Get Cooking

By offering a constant, even temperature, a Dutch oven can simmer soup to perfection, hold moisture for a flavorful roast, evenly heat oil for deep-frying, go oven-to-table for casseroles and even bake bread.

Grill Pan

A heavy-duty pan with raised ridges

The Details

Ideal for meat and vegetables, a grill pan recreates the flavorful char marks of an outdoor flame grill.

Get Cooking

Because a grill pan works best with high, dry heat, you don't need to add any butter or oil. The raised ridges allow any excess fat to drain away from the food.

Roasting Pan

A large rectangular stainless steel, cast iron, clay or aluminum pan

The Details

Using the dry heat of the oven, a roasting pan provides even heat to large or small cuts of meat and vegetables.

Get Cooking

A roasting pan can be combined with a roasting rack to cook meat above any added vegetables—the fat and juices drip to the bottom, creating rich seasoning as well as a flavorful gravy base.

Sauce Pan

A heavy-based pan with vertical or flared sides

The Details

Designed for simmering and boiling, the saucepan can be used to make sauces, poach eggs and cook grains.

Get Cooking

A saucepan with straight sides is best for heating foods with a high liquid content, while a slope-sided saucier offers quicker evaporation for sauce reduction.

Sauté Pan

A wide and heavy-bottomed pan
with a long handle

The Details

The sauté pan cooks meats and vegetables at a high heat while the ingredients are quickly tossed, a fitting method since sauté means "to jump" in French.

Get Cooking

Nonstick pans are perfect for sautéing vegetables, but a metal option creates caramelized "fond" at the bottom of the pan, which is great for a flavorful sauce base.

Skillet & Frying Pan

Flat-bottomed pan with low, flared sides

The Details

Skillets and frying pans allow for easy flipping, making it ideal for fast cooking eggs, meats and vegetables.

Get Cooking

To achieve a crispy texture and flavorful finish, start food at a medium-high heat to initiate browning, then reduce for a slow, even cook. The side you cook first will look the best, presentation-wise, so plan accordingly.

Stock Pot

Wide pot with a flat bottom, straight sides and a lid

The Details

Best for cooking with large amounts of liquid, stock pots offer tall sides to reduce evaporation in stocks and broths.

Get Cooking

One of the most common types of cooking pots worldwide, a stock pot can also be used for pasta, soups, steamed shellfish and more.

The Materials

What you're cooking on is just as important as the foods you're cooking with. Know your cast irons from your coppers and let the feasting begin.

Stainless Steel

A staple of professional kitchens the world over, stainless steel is durable and non-reactive with alkaline and acidic foods (meaning it lets your food taste the way it should). Thanks to an aluminum or copper core, stainless steel cookware also heats evenly across all sides to reduce hot spots.

Copper

An iconic symbol of high-end kitchens, copper cookware is a standout on the stove. It responds quickly to changes in temperature and gives you greater control over your food. Copper heats evenly and stops cooking your food when you take the pan off the flame. It's also a phenomenal heat conductor—the best of all metals, in fact.

Nonstick

Today's nonstick pans are specially engineered with a wide range of coatings that offer a versatile cooking surface. Beginning with Teflon in the 1950s, today's nonstick coatings include anodized aluminum, ceramic and silicone. Modern nonstick designs have thick coatings that are very durable and won't wear out.

Cast Iron

A time-honored material with excellent heat retention, cast iron can go stove to oven without a problem. Cast iron heats slowly and evenly, making it great for searing and frying as well as slow-cooking stews. Though vessels without enamel still need to be seasoned (a baked on layer of oil that prevents food from sticking), cast iron is also available with a protective enamel coating that both acts as a nonstick surface and keeps the pan from rusting.

Ceramic

Ceramic is sandblasted onto the pan's surface and then fired at 2,000˚C to create the protective coating. Free of PTFE and PFOA, ceramic cookware is a favorite of the environmentally and health conscious. It makes a great alternative to traditional nonstick pans. That's because it reduces the need for oils and fats when you cook, thereby keeping your meals just a little healthier.
