Persian vs. Oriental

Authentic Persian rugs have been handmade in Iran following traditional patterns and designs for generations. Not surprisingly, some of these designs changed as the craft spread east into India and China.

The result was Oriental rugs, which are similar in quality to Persians, but incorporate color and pattern inspiration from all over the world.

Why Knot?

Hand–knotted rugs are one of a kind. A dedicated weaver can spend months working on a single rug. This is the way they've been made for generations.

"Handmade" is a term used loosely in the rug industry. Maybe the weaver did the bulk of the work by hand, maybe someone tied it as it came off a machine. You can trust that hand-knotted rugs, though, are made by hand from start to finish (which is why most of Bloomingdale's rugs are made this way).

A Common Thread

There's a reason rugs have been made of wool for hundreds of years. Highly regarded for its strength, durability and comfort, wool is easy to clean and has a longer lifespan than other materials.

Rugs made from other fibers work for decorating, but tend to show wear (silk) or pile (polypropylene). You don't have to worry that your hand-knotted wool rug is going to fall apart after a year. Or generations (with proper care).

RUG STYLES

Traditional

Authentic Persian and Oriental styles, usually with uniform floral patterns and motifs.

Contemporary

Bold modern rugs in a variety of colorways, typically with linear or simplified repeating patterns.

Transitional

Traditional designs with modern variations in color, texture and pattern.

Quick Tips

  • – Large rugs can make a small room appear bigger
  • – Keep an area of bare floor to prevent a rug from overwhelming a space
  • – Leave equal room on all sides:

    Runners–6" on each side

    Dining room–18" beyond the edge of the table

    Bedroom–24" to the left and right sides, 24" from the back wall

    Living Room–Usually 1–2.5 feet from wall and larger than the sofa

For Good Measure

It all starts with the room. Measure the space and subtract two feet–this is the area you have to work with. It's best not to cover everything with your rug. A perimeter of hardwood floor will create a contrast that will make your rug, and room design, really pop.

Divide & Cover

Make big, open rooms feel cozy by using several different rugs to visually separate spaces.

Just like you wouldn't set two of the same paintings next to each other, you should avoid using the same rug design in a room. Rather than repetition, choose something for harmony (a unifying color, for example) to bring the design together.

Spread the Love

An easy way to add color and pattern, scatter rugs are small (typically 2' x 3' – 4' x 6') and can be placed under side tables, end tables, around a bed or in a bathroom. Feel free to experiment with smaller rugs to add classic design elements throughout your home.

PLENTY OF ROOMS

Bedroom

A worry–free way to spice up your bedroom is by placing one large rug beneath your bed. Alternatively, you can use runners and scatter rugs along the perimeter. It's best to leave a few feet from the wall your bed is up against to get that hint of contrast.

Dining Room

Any rug you place in your dining room should be larger than the table. If your table is 3' x 5', then a 3' x 5' rug is too small. Your rug should be at least 1' larger on each side. 18" is ideal.

Runners/Hallway Rugs

Placing a rug in your hallway is the perfect way to unify spaces, provide focal points and pull design elements into different areas. Hallway rugs also keep a large area from echoing, protect the floor of high traffic areas, muffle footfalls and hold in warmth by stifling drafts.

Living Room

Rule of thumb–your rug should be bigger than your sofa. That isn't to say a rug framing a coffee table in front of a fireplace is a bad idea, but a larger rug will better pull the different elements of the room together. Even just having one chair leg on the rug can create a more unified space. If your rug is especially plush or you're using a rug pad then you may need risers for some of your furniture.

Irregularly Shaped Rooms

In some irregularly shaped rooms (L–shaped, open floor plan, long and thin, etc.) an area rug can be used to define separate seating areas, working as natural room dividers for the feeling of separate spaces without walls.

Shadow Play

Because of the artisanal weaving process, the fibers of the rug naturally lean in one direction, giving each hand–knotted rug a light and dark side. When you first get your rug home, make sure to turn it around to see which tone works best for your room. Each rug on bloomingdales.com is photographed to show the darker side of the rug.

Mood Lighting

The amount of light, especially natural light, your rug receives is essential to how it appears in your home. In rooms with a lot of sunlight, a rug's colors will be muted. For a look that really stands out in a bright room, choose a rug with deep, saturated colors.

Solid Ground

Create a focal point by choosing a rug that contrasts with your floor. A light rug, or a rug with a light border, will make a big statement on a dark wood floor.

Aging Gracefully

All handmade rugs shed over the first few months. You may also have long threads pop up on your new rug. Don't panic. The rug is not coming apart. Since the knotted ends of a rug are cut to create the finished surface, curled threads are sometimes missed and naturally straighten later. This is called sprouting. You can cut the threads to the same height as the rest of the rug without damaging it.

Maintenance Tips

The more you care for your rug, the better it will hold up. Modern rugs made from cheaper materials will wear out and need to be replaced frequently, but if you take care of a hand–knotted rug, it can last generations.

- Spills and stains need to be addressed quickly. Moisture is your rug's biggest enemy. Use a clean white towel or paper towel to soak up spills. Do not rub. Then clean the spot with lukewarm water and blot up any excess liquid.

- Vacuuming your rug once a week is recommended to help prevent build–up. Removing your vacuum's beater bar and vacuuming in the direction of the pile can help prevent damage from the vacuum.

- It helps to shake out rugs every month to remove dust and grime that a vacuum might miss.

- Rotate the rug 180 degrees once a year to ensure it ages evenly.

A

Aniline Dyes

Synthetic dyes made from coal tar used prior to early 1900's.

C

Cartouche

A design that surrounds a woven signature, date or inscription found in authentic Persian rugs.

Chrome Dyes

Synthetic dyes used in modern rug production.

F

Field

The part of the rug that is surrounded by the border. An open field refers to a rug that contains minimal background pattern.

Flat-Weave

Generic classification of rugs that have flat piles; e.g. Soumaks, Kilims, Dhurries.

Foundation

The warp (vertical) and the weft (horizontal) threads on a loom that are the foundation of the rug.

I

Ikat

Originally a dyeing technique, this refers to rugs that are inspired by these patterned textiles.

K

Kilim

A flat rug with no pile; now found in a variety of patterns and colors ranging from tribals to solids.

Knot

Used to weave a handmade rug. It's formed when the yarn is looped around the threads. The majority of handmade rugs are woven using a Persian, Turkish or Tibetan knot.

L

Loom

A structure used to weave rugs. It can be a simple wooden loom or a massive power loom used to weave machine–made rugs.

M

Medallion

A prominent design in the very center of the rug's field. Persian patterns with a center medallion are referred to as a Tabriz.

Moroccan

Tribal, minimalistic Moroccan–inspired designs usually found in neutrals with deep piles.

N

Nap

Face of the rug where the knot ends are cut. In handmade rugs you have the light (with the nap) and the dark side (against the nap).

O

Oriental Rug

Rugs woven in Pakistan, India and China that follow the Persian rug tradition, typically with variations in design and color.

P

Patina

The surface appearance of a rug which usually mellows with age or use. It can also be achieved through the use of rug washes.

Persian Rug

Rugs woven in Iran, formerly Persia, utilizing centuries–old techniques, patterns and colors.

Pile

A rug's visual surface, which consists of loops or cut knot ends; also known as the face or nap.

S

Sprouting

When a loose end of yarn pops up to the surface of a rug; they should be cut with scissors.

T

Tribal

A generic term used to describe a simple, traditional geometric rug.

W

Warp

The beginning part of a rug where the threads are attached to a loom vertically.

Weft

Strands inserted horizontally over and under the warp to create the foundation of the rug.
